Purpose

The purpose of this BrainLift is to get a real handle on pretraining optimization for the eduLLM — not just “use AdamW because everyone does,” but understand why momentum / adaptive methods work, where the theory says you can’t do better, and what the current frontier (AdamW, μP, Schedule-Free, Muon) is actually changing. I’m specializing here so that when we train the ~1B testbed, I can argue for a setup that fits curriculum-shaped data and the “learning gain per token / mastery per dollar” bar, instead of copying a web-crawl recipe.

In scope. Momentum / first-order theory (mostly Distill, which I read end-to-end). AdamW as the null. Frontier map for μP, Schedule-Free, Muon — claim + what I’d measure. Coupling to P1-style curriculum data. What I’d actually run on the shared 1B testbed.

Out of scope. Post-training (except as something that eats the checkpoint). Tokenizer work (P2). Full memory-split architecture (P3), except when it shows up as a geometry hypothesis. Pretending I’ve deep-read every paper below — Distill is the deep read. The rest are mapped so I know what to falsify.

DOK4 — Spiky Points of View

SPOV 1: Changing the geometry matters more than inventing a slightly smarter stepsize.

Distill’s punchline is that on a fixed hard loss, first-order methods (linear combos of past gradients) basically can’t beat optimally tuned momentum. The escape hatch they leave open is: reformulate so the condition number itself changes. That’s the part that maps onto Incept. We’re not training on shuffled internet sludge. We’re trying to build textbook / skill-DAG / worked-example data on purpose. So the interesting “optimization” lever might be the corpus + parametrization + update metric, not hunting a magic $\beta$. I’m calling this κ-first for myself: shape $\kappa$ (and the geometry) before you obsess over $\alpha$.

SPOV 2: Pretraining should match the kind of learning we want — structured textbook knowledge for teaching, not general web perplexity.

I also think the loss + optimizer stack should depend on the fact that this is an eduLLM. Priority is a model that teaches well under a tiny budget, not a mini GPT clone. Learning-science sequencing during pretraining isn’t just a nice P1 idea. In Distill language, ordering and connectivity change which modes get gradient mass early. Skill-DAG / worked examples / QA augmentation might be acting like spectral interventions, not only like “teaching the model like a student.”

SPOV 3: AdamW is the null. Muon (and friends) only count if they buy a multiplier on our harness.

There’s a lot of hype around Muon and other geometry-ish optimizers. Fine. On the 1B testbed, AdamW is still the thing you have to beat on learning gain per token and mastery per dollar, with CIs. “Paper said ~2×” is a prior, not a deliverable. Same for Schedule-Free: cool if it cuts tuning tax, irrelevant if eval doesn’t move.

SPOV 4: Under our token budgets, the transient phase matters more than the asymptotic noise floor.

Distill + the older momentum-in-deep-learning line both point at this: early / mid training is where useful progress happens, and noise can act like a regularizer, but huge $\beta$ also compounds noise through the impulse response. For sovereign-scale token counts, I’d rather pick optimizers / stop rules by tokens-to-target on educational evals than by how pretty train loss looks at the end.

DOK3 — Insights

  • On a nice convex quadratic in the eigenbasis, each direction updates on its own: $x_i^{k+1}=(1-\alpha\lambda_i)x_i^k$. That’s why pathological curvature / $\kappa$ shows up at all — slow modes dominate.
  • Momentum turns each mode into a 2D system (velocity + position). Optimal $(\alpha,\beta)$ is about balancing stiffest and softest modes. For bad $\kappa$, $\beta$ wants to sit near 1.
  • Distill’s path / “Convex Rosenbrock” makes the lower bound match momentum’s rate as $n\to\infty$. On that class, you can’t first-order your way past $q=(\sqrt{\kappa}-1)/(\sqrt{\kappa}+1)$. Spiritually: don’t expect a clever $\gamma$ combo to save a terrible geometry.
  • Early stopping on GD looks a lot like ridge / Tikhonov. Feels related to schema modes vs noisy fact modes, especially next to P3.
  • AdamW’s big deal is less “new intelligence” and more “don’t couple weight decay into the adaptive step wrong.” Still the practical null.
  • μP is the width version of “get the geometry right before you tune LR.”
  • Muon is Distill’s escape hatch with engineering attached: change update geometry instead of staying in plain Euclidean Adam steps. Hypothesis, not a fact for our corpus yet.
  • Schedule-Free attacks a different pain: not knowing $T$ / burning time on schedules. For hackathon-scale ablations that might matter as much as step time.
  • Edge of Stability warns classical $\kappa$ stories don’t fully describe deep nets. I’m not pretending Distill numbers export 1:1 into Transformers. The intuition still helps me ask better questions.
  • Noise in stochastic momentum isn’t free. Large $\beta$ can make old gradient noise linger.

Bottleneck math (the Distill spine)

GD vs momentum rates on condition number $\kappa$:

$$ \rho_{\mathrm{GD}}\approx\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}, \qquad \rho_{\mathrm{HB}}\approx\frac{\sqrt{\kappa}-1}{\sqrt{\kappa}+1}. $$

First-order class:

$$ w^{k+1}=w^{0}+\sum_{i}\gamma_i^{k}\,\nabla f(w^{i}). $$

On the hard path, info moves one hop per step, so you get a matching lower bound with that same $q=(\sqrt{\kappa}-1)/(\sqrt{\kappa}+1)$. Full derivations are in the math notes.

DOK2 — Knowledge tree (compressed)

1. Distill (deep-read)

Facts. Eigenbasis decoupling. $\kappa$ as pathological curvature. Momentum state $(y,x)$. Optimal rate $\sqrt{\kappa}$ form. Path lower bound. Stochastic kicks (and Distill’s displayed noise formula is sloppy — corrected in notes). Escape hatch: change $\kappa$, don’t only remix gradients.

Summary. Eigenbasis makes GD/momentum less mysterious. $\sqrt{\kappa}$ speedup is real and basically tight for linear first-order methods on that worst-case class. Useful takeaway for eduLLM: if the geometry is cursed, first-order cleverness won’t save you — change the geometry.

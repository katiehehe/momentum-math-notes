Purpose

This BrainLift develops a working thesis on pretraining optimization for Incept’s eduLLM: why classical momentum theory constrains first-order methods, where AdamW sits as the practical baseline, and how frontier methods (μP, Schedule-Free, Muon) should be interpreted as interventions on update geometry rather than as drop-in folklore.

The goal is to recommend a measurable pretraining stack for the shared ~1B testbed — one that fits curriculum-shaped data and is judged by learning gain per token and mastery per dollar.

In scope. First-order / momentum theory (primary source: Distill [1], read in full). AdamW as null. Frontier map for μP, Schedule-Free, and Muon with falsification criteria. Coupling between curriculum data design (P1) and optimization geometry. Experiment plan on the 1B testbed.

Out of scope. Post-training, except as a downstream consumer of the checkpoint. Tokenizer design (P2). Full memory-split architecture (P3), except as a related capacity/geometry hypothesis. Exhaustive review of every optimizer variant.

DOK4 — Spiky Points of View

SPOV 1 — Geometry is the primary optimization lever for curriculum-shaped eduLLM pretraining.

On a fixed smooth strongly convex problem, optimally tuned momentum is essentially tight among linear first-order methods: Distill’s path construction matches the $(\sqrt{\kappa}-1)/(\sqrt{\kappa}+1)$ rate from above and below [1]. The constructive implication is not that momentum research is finished, but that further gains require changing problem geometry — condition number, parametrization, or update metric. Incept is already attempting that change on the data side (textbooks, skill graphs, worked examples). Pretraining optimization should therefore be framed as κ-first design: shape effective geometry, then select the optimizer that exploits it. Hyperparameter defaults built for shuffled web-crawl landscapes are the wrong starting point.

SPOV 2 — Optimizer choice should be conditional on the educational objective, not on generic LM perplexity norms.

An eduLLM is trained to support teaching under a constrained budget. That objective changes which training regimes matter: schema acquisition, transfer, and tokens-to-harness-threshold dominate asymptotic train loss on an infinite crawl. Learning-science sequencing is therefore not only a data-quality intervention. In Distill’s eigenmode language, ordering and connectivity affect which modes receive gradient mass early. Skill-DAG structure, worked examples, and QA augmentation should be treated as candidate spectral interventions and measured against shuffled controls under a fixed optimizer.

SPOV 3 — AdamW remains the null hypothesis. Frontier optimizers must purchase a harness multiplier.

AdamW [2][3] is the correct baseline for LLM pretraining. Muon [6], Schedule-Free [5], and related methods are admissible only as measured alternatives on the shared testbed: learning gain per token and mastery per dollar, with confidence intervals. Published speedups are priors. They are not deliverables.

SPOV 4 — Under sovereign token budgets, transient progress dominates asymptotic noise-floor optimality.

Distill’s stochastic analysis and the classical momentum literature [1][7] emphasize that deep learning often lives in a transient regime where noise can regularize, while large momentum can also retain noise through a slowly decaying impulse response. For limited-token educational pretraining, selection criteria should prioritize tokens-to-target on educational evaluations over late-stage training-loss polish.

DOK3 — Insights

  1. In the eigenbasis of a convex quadratic, gradient descent decouples across modes, $x_i^{k+1}=(1-\alpha\lambda_i)x_i^k$. Pathological curvature is the spread of those $\lambda_i$ (condition number $\kappa$).
  2. Momentum lifts each mode to a two-dimensional linear system. Optimal $(\alpha,\beta)$ equalizes behavior at $\lambda_{\min}$ and $\lambda_{\max}$. Ill-conditioned problems push $\beta$ toward 1.
  3. Distill’s path lower bound shows that no method of the form $w^{k+1}=w^0+\sum\gamma_i^k\nabla f(w^i)$ can asymptotically beat momentum’s rate on that family as $n\to\infty$. Practical reading: coefficient cleverness will not repair a bad geometry.
  4. Early stopping under GD is qualitatively Tikhonov-like. Useful analogy for schema-versus-fact capacity allocation (adjacent to P3).
  5. AdamW’s lasting contribution is geometric and regularizer hygiene — especially decoupled weight decay — not a new loss.
  6. μP [4] is width-wise geometry control: stable update magnitudes make learning-rate transfer possible.
  7. Muon [6] is Distill’s escape hatch made algorithmic: alter the update metric rather than remaining in Euclidean Adam geometry. A hypothesis to test on Incept data.
  8. Schedule-Free [5] targets tuning cost (horizon / schedule dependence). On ablation-heavy budgets, that can matter as much as wall-clock per step.
  9. Edge of Stability [8] limits literal export of classical $\kappa$ theory into deep nets. Spectral intuition remains a design language, not an exact Transformer rate calculator.
  10. In the corrected stochastic unroll, large $\beta$ slows decay of the position impulse response, so momentum can improve conditioning while worsening noise retention.

Bottleneck mathematics

GD versus momentum contraction factors:

$$ \rho_{\mathrm{GD}}\approx\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}, \qquad \rho_{\mathrm{HB}}\approx\frac{\sqrt{\kappa}-1}{\sqrt{\kappa}+1}. $$

First-order class and the information constraint motivating Distill’s lower bound:

$$ w^{k+1}=w^{0}+\sum_{i}\gamma_i^{k}\,\nabla f(w^{i}). $$

DOK2 — Knowledge tree

1. Distill (completed deep read) [1]

Facts. Eigenbasis decoupling. $\kappa$ as pathological curvature. Momentum state $(y,x)$ with step matrix $R$. Optimal rate $(\sqrt{\kappa}-1)/(\sqrt{\kappa}+1)$. Path objective $f^n$ with $w_i^\star=q^i$ and $\kappa(f^n)\to\kappa$. First-order span constraint. Stochastic impulse-response variance (Distill’s single-$\varepsilon^k$ display is incorrect — corrected in appendix). Explicit escape hatch: change $\kappa$.

Summary. Distill explains both why momentum helps and why first-order methods stall on worst-case geometry. For eduLLM pretraining, the transferable conclusion is methodological: treat geometry (data, parametrization, update metric) as the design object, and treat AdamW-class methods as the measurement baseline.

Experiment plan

  1. Establish AdamW null on the shared corpus.
  2. Hold optimizer fixed. Ablate shuffled versus skill-ordered / textbook-structured data.
  3. Introduce μP from a width proxy before any Muon comparison.
  4. Compare Muon versus AdamW only after steps 2–3.
  5. Primary metric: tokens-to-harness-threshold and \$/mastery. Train loss secondary.

Run invariant. Record optimizer, corpus-geometry tag, parametrization tag, tokens, harness scores with CIs, and approximate dollar cost.

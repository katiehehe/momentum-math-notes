Geometry Before Stepsize

Pretraining optimization for curriculum-shaped eduLLMs

Katie He · Owners: Katie He
Companion: Why Momentum Works — math notes · Live: GitHub Pages

Thesis

The ideal pretraining system for Incept’s ~1B eduLLM should not treat AdamW + cosine as a settled default borrowed from web-scale crawls. Those recipes assume a messy, high-entropy data geometry. We are deliberately building the opposite: a curriculum-shaped corpus (textbooks, worked examples, skill-DAG orderings) whose purpose is to change what the model sees—and therefore which directions in parameter space are hard.

My central bet:

First-order methods are already near-optimal on a fixed loss geometry. The eduLLM win is to change that geometry—then only secondarily pick the optimizer that exploits it.

I call this stance κ-first pretraining: treat effective condition number / update geometry as a first-class design object, not a hyperparameter afterthought. Distill’s momentum analysis is the mathematical spine of that claim [1]. The frontier stack—AdamW, μP, Schedule-Free, Muon—is the map of how people change geometry in practice [2]–[6]. The Incept constraint—learning gain per token and mastery per dollar on a shared 1B testbed—is what makes the claim falsifiable rather than aesthetic.

Change $\kappa$ before you tune $\alpha$.

Bottleneck

On a quadratic with condition number $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$, optimally tuned gradient descent contracts errors like

$$ \rho_{\mathrm{GD}} \approx \frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}, $$

while optimally tuned momentum contracts like

$$ \rho_{\mathrm{HB}} \approx \frac{\sqrt{\kappa}-1}{\sqrt{\kappa}+1}. $$

For large $\kappa$, GD needs $\Theta(\kappa\log(1/\varepsilon))$ steps; momentum needs $\Theta(\sqrt{\kappa}\log(1/\varepsilon))$. That $\sqrt{\kappa}$ speedup is real—and Distill shows it is also essentially tight for linear first-order methods on worst-case smooth strongly convex problems [1]. The escape hatch in the same article is the one that matters for us:

You cannot invent a cleverer combination of past gradients to beat the lower bound on a fixed hard $f$. You can reformulate so $\kappa$ itself changes.

For a sovereign eduLLM, FLOPs are scarce and tokens are curated. The bottleneck is not “find a slightly better $\beta$.” It is: every token of Skill-DAG / worked-example / QA-augmented data is an intervention on the spectrum of the training problem. Optimizer research that ignores that is optimizing the wrong object.

Operationally, every pretraining report should quote two currencies the program already demands:

  1. Learning gain per token (eval harness, with CIs).
  2. Mastery per dollar (same gains divided by H100-hours × price).

An optimizer “win” that does not move those numbers is not a win.

Mechanism: κ-first pretraining

Pipeline I want measured on the shared testbed:

$$ \text{corpus geometry} \;\rightarrow\; \text{parametrization ($\mu$P)} \;\rightarrow\; \text{update geometry (AdamW / Muon / Schedule-Free)} \;\rightarrow\; \text{schedule / budget} \;\rightarrow\; \text{harness multipliers}. $$

Layer A — Data as spectral design (P1 coupling)

Curriculum levers (QA augmentation, worked examples, skill-DAG order, RHO-1-style selection) are usually sold as pedagogy in the corpus. κ-first reframes them as mode schedulers:

  • In the eigenbasis of a quadratic, each mode $i$ evolves independently: $x_i^{k+1}=(1-\alpha\lambda_i)x_i^k$ [1].
  • Large-$\lambda$ modes fit fast and are robust; small-$\lambda$ modes are slow and noise-sensitive.
  • Early stopping ≈ Tikhonov: you leave small modes underfit on purpose.

A skill-ordered textbook corpus is a hypothesis that we can front-load informative, well-conditioned updates (schema / procedure modes) and defer or externalize brittle fact modes (the P3 memory-split bet). That is Distill’s soft-vs-stiff language applied to educational data design.

Layer B — Parametrization before optimizer (μP)

Maximal Update Parametrization [4] is the cleanest industrial instance of “change geometry before you tune stepsizes”: transfer learning rates from a small model to a large one because the update scale was designed to be width-stable. For a 1B testbed that will later jump to production width, μP is how we stop confusing “AdamW is unstable” with “we scaled LR wrong.”

The Hard Part

1. Distill’s lower bound, stated cleanly

Any method of the form

$$ w^{k+1}=w^{0}+\sum_{i=0}^{k}\gamma_i^{k}\,\nabla f(w^{i}) $$

only moves in the span of gradients seen so far. On Distill’s path (“Convex Rosenbrock”) $f^n$, information hops one edge per step. Starting at $0$, after $k$ steps coordinates $i\ge k+1$ are untouched, so

$$ \|w^{k}-w^{\star}\|_{\infty} \;\ge\; q^{k}\,\|w^{0}-w^{\star}\|_{\infty}, \qquad q=\frac{\sqrt{\kappa}-1}{\sqrt{\kappa}+1}. $$

As $n\to\infty$, $\kappa(f^n)\to\kappa$, and this lower bound meets momentum’s upper bound. On that problem class, you cannot first-order your way past $q$.

Spiritually: optimality of momentum is a warning against clever $\gamma$-hacking. It is not a stop sign for Muon, Fisher/natural gradient, or curriculum redesign—those leave the algorithm class or change $f$.

2. Stochastic phase vs transient phase

With gradient noise, the mode error splits as

$$ \mathbf{E}\,f(x^{k}) = \frac{\lambda}{2}\bigl(e_2^{\top}R^{k}s^{0}\bigr)^{2} + \frac{\lambda\sigma^{2}}{2}\sum_{\ell=0}^{k-1}\bigl(e_2^{\top}R^{\ell}v\bigr)^{2}, $$

deterministic transient plus a noise floor filtered by the impulse response of $R$ [1]. Distill + Sutskever et al. [7] emphasize that in deep learning the transient often matters more than the fine-tuning noise floor. For Incept’s token budgets, that is decisive: we should optimize for fast useful progress on schema-heavy evals, not for asymptotic stationary variance on an infinite crawl.

3. What I am still unsure about

Whether textbook/skill-DAG data actually improves effective conditioning in a way Muon can exploit—or whether the gains are purely from better targets (facts in QA form) with AdamW already saturating the geometry. That is empirical. κ-first says we should instrument for it (proxy sharpness, update-size histograms, tokens-to-harness-threshold), not assume it.

DOK 4 — Spiky Points of View

SPOV 1 — Geometry is the product; the optimizer is packaging

Claim. For curriculum-shaped eduLLM pretraining, the highest-leverage “optimization” work is changing effective $\kappa$ (data order, mixture, parametrization, update metric)—not inventing a new adaptive $\varepsilon$.

Why spiky. Most LLM lore starts from AdamW and debates schedules. Distill’s closure says first-order methods are already tight given $f$. Incept’s P1 thesis says we are rewriting $f$ on purpose. Those two sentences force a reordering of the research stack.

Implication. Every optimizer ablation must be paired with a corpus-geometry ablation. Muon-on-DCLM-shuffle answers a different question than Muon-on-Skill-DAG-textbooks.

SPOV 2 — Transient > asymptote under sovereign token budgets

Claim. On the 1B testbed, select optimizers/schedules by tokens-to-target on educational evals in the early and middle training regime, even if asymptotic train loss looks worse.

Why. Noise-floor theory and infinite-horizon schedules are written for regimes we are not buying. Learning science cares when schemas stabilize, not whether epoch 200 is $10^{-4}$ lower.

SPOV 3 — Learning-science sequencing is a spectral intervention

Claim. Skill-DAG / worked-example / QA ordering should be analyzed as shaping which eigenmodes receive gradient mass early—not only as “teaching the model like a student.”

Why. Distill’s colorization / path examples show locality and connectivity control conditioning. A prerequisite graph in the corpus is connectivity design on concepts. This bridges cognitive science (P1) into optimization theory without pretending Sweller invented AdamW.

SPOV 4 — AdamW remains the null; Muon must buy a multiplier

Claim. AdamW [2], [3] is the correct null hypothesis for Incept pretraining. Muon [6] and Schedule-Free [5] are licensed only as measured alternatives: tokens-to-harness and \$/mastery with CIs. “Paper said 2×” is not a deliverable.

Why. The program’s prime directive is the race car. Frontier papers are proposals for which car to put on the track.

DOK 3 — Insights

  1. Independence in the eigenbasis is why momentum theory works at all. Once $A=Q\Lambda Q^{\top}$, GD and heavy-ball decouple per mode [1]. Adaptive diagonal methods buy a cheap online version when $Q$ is unknown.
  2. AdamW’s real contribution is geometric + regularizer hygiene: decoupled weight decay [3] fixes how $L_2$ interacted with adaptive steps.
  3. μP is the width-wise cousin of κ-first [4]: stable update magnitudes across width prevent mistuning $\alpha$ when the 1B recipe promotes.
  4. Muon is Distill’s escape hatch with engineering attached [6]: a bet that Euclidean Adam steps fight the wrong metric. If true on our corpus, tokens-to-target moves; if false, we publish the null.
  5. Schedule-Free attacks tuning tax [5]: for hackathon-scale ablations, not knowing $T$ in advance may matter as much as wall-clock per step.
  6. Stochastic momentum compounds noise when $\beta$ is large because the impulse response $\gamma_\ell=e_2^{\top}R^{\ell}v$ decays slowly [1].
  7. Edge of Stability [8] warns classical $\kappa$ theory is incomplete for deep nets. I treat it as a limit on exporting Distill numbers literally into Transformers—not as a reason to discard spectral intuition.

SLMs / Why this matters at ~1B

At 1B parameters and thousands of H100-hours, you cannot brute-force past a bad geometry. Chinchilla-optimal token counts [9] still assume you spend tokens well; P1 says many internet tokens are the wrong tokens. κ-first pretraining keeps optimization specialization loyal to the eduLLM thesis: maximize schooling utility per dollar, not WikiText against a 405B.

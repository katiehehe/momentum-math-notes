Hessian matrix geometry

The Hessian $H$ is the matrix of second derivatives of a scalar loss $L(\theta)$:

$$ H_{ij}=\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial\theta_i\,\partial\theta_j} $$

In matrix form:

$$ H(\theta)=\nabla^2 L(\theta) $$

Newton update uses the inverse Hessian:

$$ \theta\leftarrow\theta-H^{-1}\nabla L $$

Can have positive and negative eigenvalues (convex vs saddle regions). Purely geometric: how the loss bends around a point.

Fisher information matrix probability

For a model $p_\theta(y\mid x)$, the Fisher is the covariance of the score:

$$ F=\mathbb{E}_{x,y\sim p_\theta}\!\Bigl[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(y\mid x)\, \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(y\mid x)^\top \Bigr] $$

Equivalently (under regularity conditions):

$$ F=-\mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\nabla^2_\theta\log p_\theta(y\mid x)\bigr] $$

Natural gradient uses the inverse Fisher:

$$ \theta\leftarrow\theta-F^{-1}\nabla L $$

Always positive semi-definite. Measures how sensitive the distribution is to parameter changes.

When they agree (NLL / MLE) link

For negative log-likelihood loss

$$ L(\theta)=-\log p_\theta(y\mid x) $$

the expected Hessian of $L$ equals the Fisher:

$$ \mathbb{E}\bigl[\nabla^2 L(\theta)\bigr]=F $$

So for MLE they often match in expectation. In practice people estimate Fisher from score outer products (gradients), not from full second derivatives.

Rule of thumb: Hessian = “how curved is my loss?” Fisher = “how much does changing $\theta$ change the predictive distribution?”
